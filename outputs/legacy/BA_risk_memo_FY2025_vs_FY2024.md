## Analyst Narrative

**Dominant theme: codification, not escalation.** FY2025 is a *rewriting* year for Boeing's risk disclosures, not an *expansion* year. Eighteen of 28 risk factors (~64%) were materially touched, yet the company added only one net new risk and removed five. That ratio matters: when management rewrites two-thirds of its risk language without expanding the surface area, the disclosures are usually being updated to reflect *resolved or now-permanent* situations rather than newly recognized threats. The category mix supports this read — operational is the only category with meaningful churn (3 removed, 8 modified), and most of those modifications are reframings of existing exposure (Spirit AeroSystems integration, 737 production posture, fixed-price defense programs) rather than fresh disclosures.

**The three modifications that carry the most signal.** First, the *Liquidity* risk (financial, classified HIGH severity) was rewritten to drop the FY2024 framing of "Net cash used by operating activities was $12.1 billion" in favor of a hard balance-sheet disclosure: "As of December 31, 2025, our debt totaled $54.1 billion, of which approximately $15.5 billion of principal" — a shift from cash-burn narrative to debt-stock narrative is consistent with operating cash flow having improved while the leverage overhang remains, though confirming that requires the financial statements (analyst inference). Second, the *Commercial Airplanes production* risk (operational, HIGH) is now anchored to a specific named event — "the 737-9 door plug accident in January 2024" — and to "slowed 737 production rates and delayed planned production rate increases." Naming the incident in the risk factors formalizes a production ceiling that was previously discussed only in MD&A, which generally signals the constraint is expected to persist into the disclosure year. Third, Boeing reframed its trade exposure: a standalone *supply_chain* risk about non-U.S. trade authority disputes was removed, and the only **new** risk for FY2025 — explicitly listing "tariffs, duties... embargoes, sanctions" — was added under the *geopolitical* category, with the related modified geopolitical risk now noting "Current U.S. trade policy includes the imposition of baseline, sectoral" tariffs. The same underlying exposure was elevated from operational/supply-chain framing to macro/geopolitical framing, suggesting management views tariff risk as policy-driven rather than supplier-driven going forward.

**Red-flag scan came back clean across all three years — read carefully.** The detector found zero instances of "going concern," "material weakness," "restatement," or "SEC investigation" in Risk Factors or MD&A for FY2023, FY2024, or FY2025. This is genuinely informative: despite the high-profile 737-9 incident, FAA scrutiny, and large operating losses, Boeing's auditors and counsel have not required the specific phrase set that typically accompanies an existential disclosure event. That said, the absence of those tokens does *not* clear the underlying issues — the HIGH-severity classifier flags on Liquidity, Commercial Airplanes production, defense fixed-price programs, geopolitical/trade, and cyber/IT infrastructure all show that the substance of the concerns is intact, just expressed in conventional risk-factor language rather than red-flag boilerplate.

**Bottom-line directional read: stable, with a tilt toward improvement on the financial-narrative side and ongoing pressure on the operational side.** The risk profile is *not* deteriorating year-over-year: net risk count is flat (28 vs. 29), no new red-flag language has appeared, the Spirit acquisition has moved from "pending deal" risk to "closed-deal dilution" risk (a normal post-close transition), and trade exposure has been recategorized rather than amplified. The pressure points worth watching are (a) the $54.1B debt disclosure now sitting in a HIGH-severity liquidity risk, (b) the explicit codification of slower 737 production rates as ongoing rather than transitional, and (c) the new geopolitical/tariff risk as a macro overlay that Boeing did not previously frame as a discrete risk factor. Net: this looks like a company that is normalizing post-crisis disclosures rather than disclosing fresh trouble — improving in tone, but with the underlying operational and leverage constraints fully preserved.
